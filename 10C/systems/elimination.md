## Solving Systems by Elimination
### Elimination: First Encounter
Consider the system

\[
\begin{cases}
x - 3y = 5 \\
4x + 3y = 10
\end{cases}
\]

The coefficients of \(y\) are opposites: \(-3\) and \(+3\).  
So if we add the equations, the \(y\)-terms cancel.

\[
\begin{aligned}
(x - 3y) + (4x + 3y) &= 5 + 10 \\
(x +4x) + (-3y + 3y) &= 15 \\
5x &= 15
\end{aligned}
\]

Now solve for \(x\):

\[
\begin{aligned}
5x &= 15 \\
x &= 3
\end{aligned}
\]

Now that we've solved for \(x\), all we have to do is solve for \(y\). Take the first equation and substitute $x=3$ back in:

\[\begin{aligned}
(3)-3y &= 5\\
-3 &\quad -3\\
-3y &= 2\\
\div -3 &\quad \div-3\\
y &= -\frac{2}{3}
.\end{aligned}\] 

### Elimination: A bit harder
What happens when our equations don't line up as nicely? In this example, we have two equations where the $y$ terms have different coefficients! 

\[
\begin{cases}
    y+3x = 5\\
    -2y +4x =10
\end{cases}
.\] 

In this example, all we have to do is multiply both sides of the first equation by $2$. This gives us a new system:

\[
\begin{cases}
    2y+6x = 10\\
    -2y +4x =10
\end{cases}
.\] 

Now our $y$-coefficients match up! We can add our equations together, and get:

\[
\begin{aligned}
    (2y+6x)+(-2y+4x)&=10+10\\
    (2y-2y)+(6x+4x)&= 20 \\
    10x&= 20 \\
    x&= 2
\end{aligned}
.\]
